Approach & Architecture

How It Works

Each listing is analyzed once at ingestion time, not per user query. This keeps search fast and costs predictable.

  1. Listing text (title, description, amenities) is parsed for keywords and structured data
  2. Up to 10 photos per listing are sent to Claude Haiku in a single batch request
  3. The model returns a structured JSON response with all requested trait detections
  4. Results are stored in the database as a JSON column on the listing record
  5. Users can then filter/search by any detected trait instantly
All selected traits are analyzed in a single API call per listing (photos batched together). Adding more traits has minimal marginal cost since the images are already being processed. The primary cost driver is the number of photos, not the number of traits.

Model Recommendation

Claude 3.5 Haiku is the recommended model for this pipeline:

  • Vision capability with good spatial understanding
  • Fast enough for batch processing (sub-second per listing)
  • Cost-effective at ~$0.002/listing (10 photos)
  • Structured JSON output support reduces parsing errors
  • Good at following detailed classification rubrics

For listings where higher accuracy is needed (e.g., luxury properties, featured listings), Claude 3.5 Sonnet can be used as a fallback at ~5x the cost (~$0.01/listing).

Challenges & Limitations

Photo Quality & Coverage

  • Limited angles: Most listings show 5-15 photos, often missing rooms entirely. Bathrooms and closets are frequently not photographed.
  • Staging vs. reality: Staged apartments may show furniture and decor that won't be present. Virtual staging is increasingly common and can be misleading.
  • Wide-angle distortion: Real estate photographers use ultra-wide lenses that make rooms appear larger. The model cannot determine actual room dimensions from photos.
  • Lighting tricks: HDR photography can mask dark rooms. A room that appears bright and airy in photos may face a brick wall in reality.
  • Selective photography: Landlords don't photograph problems. Missing photos of a room often means that room is unappealing.

Model Limitations

  • Hallucination risk: Vision models sometimes "see" things that aren't there, especially with ambiguous or low-quality images. Confidence calibration is imperfect.
  • Cannot detect: Noise levels, smells, temperature, water pressure, cell signal, actual dimensions, neighborhood safety, landlord responsiveness.
  • Material confusion: Laminate vs. real hardwood, granite vs. quartz, real vs. faux brick are difficult to distinguish in photos.
  • Temporal blindness: Photos may be months or years old. Seasonal elements (light, foliage) may not reflect current conditions.
  • Occlusion: Furniture blocks view of floors, walls, outlets, and fixtures. A couch may hide damaged flooring or missing baseboards.

Text Extraction Caveats

  • Inconsistent terminology: "Ensuite" vs "en-suite" vs "attached bath". "W/D in unit" vs "washer dryer" vs "laundry in apt".
  • Exaggeration: Listing descriptions are marketing copy. "Sun-drenched" may mean one window faces south. "Spacious" is subjective.
  • Missing info ≠ absence: A listing not mentioning a dishwasher doesn't mean there isn't one. Text extraction has high false-negative rates for unlisted features.
  • Abbreviations & typos: "BR" "bdrm" "bd" all mean bedroom. Text parsing must handle extensive abbreviation tables.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Use confidence scores — only surface traits with high confidence to users
  • Combine image + text signals when possible (cross-validation)
  • Mark traits as "detected" vs "not detected" rather than "present" vs "absent"
  • Allow users to correct/override detected traits after viewing
  • Run periodic audits on a sample of listings to measure actual accuracy
